# ADR-006: v7.76 "Anchor" — Archival-Grade Records and Provable Replica Derivation

**Status:** Proposed (v7.76, Phase D in design)

**Supersedes:** None. Extends ADR-005 (v7.75 "Provenance") with new schema primitives and lifecycle composition.

**Related:** ADR-001 (four-part legitimacy test), ADR-002 (BuildSchemaEntry structured parameters), ADR-003 (caller-provides, SDK-validates pattern), ADR-004 (zero-trust scope resolution), ADR-005 (cryptographic locks and mutation-audit discipline).

---

## Context

v7.76 "Anchor" adds the protocol-level primitives required to support archival-grade record systems: records whose contents are committed-to on-log without ever appearing in public storage, replicas that are cryptographically chained to their masters under provable derivation, and authorization grants that bind specific parties to specific operations against specific subjects with explicit expiration semantics.

The motivating use cases are domain applications that require strong archival secrecy combined with public auditability: vital records (birth certificates, marriage certificates, death certificates), sealed court records, restricted property filings, and regulated credentialing events. Each of these domains needs the same combination of properties — record contents protected against retroactive cryptographic breaks, but the existence and structure of records publicly verifiable from log data alone.

v7.76 does not invent new cryptography. Every primitive in this release composes existing v7.75 cryptographic foundations: Pedersen VSS for threshold escrow, AES-GCM for symmetric encryption, ECIES for key wrapping, secp256k1 ECDSA for institutional signatures, and the universal `LengthPrefixed` rule for content commitments. The novelty is in the composition: how records, authorizations, and replicas are linked on-log to make their relationships verifiable by any party walking the public log.

v7.76 is scoped as a complete SDK release. There is no companion phase that finishes operator-side adoption. The SDK ships the primitives; operators wire them into their existing admission, persistence, and query infrastructure using documented interfaces. Domain applications instantiate the primitives for their specific record types using documented composition patterns. The boundary between SDK, operator, and domain is sharp and stated explicitly in the scope boundaries section.

The prior consideration of metering, billing, and operator-economics primitives — which surfaced during v7.76 design — is explicitly excluded from this ADR. The protocol records cryptographic facts; external services consume those facts to compute whatever they need for their own purposes. This is the same discipline ADR-005 established with specification-in-source for cryptographic primitives, applied to operator economics: the SDK is neutral on prices, customer registries, and billing models. Operators implement their own metering against the public log.

## Decision

v7.76 comprises seven locked commitments. Each is specified in source; this ADR records the decision and the boundary.

### 1. Schema fingerprint discipline binds entries to schema bytes.

Every entry that carries a `SchemaRef` field gains a 32-byte `SchemaFingerprint` field. The fingerprint is computed at entry construction time via `crypto/hash.LengthPrefixed("ORTHOLOG-V7.76-SCHEMA-FINGERPRINT-v1", schema_canonical_bytes)`. The verifier independently fetches the schema at the referenced log position, recomputes the fingerprint, and rejects mismatch.

This discipline closes a gap in v7.75: while `SchemaRef` already pins a schema by log position, the verifier has no on-log binding from "the entry that references this schema" to "the specific bytes the schema contained at the time of reference." A schema-substitution attack — where an attacker modifies the schema bytes after records reference it — currently goes undetected. The fingerprint makes the binding cryptographic.

Backward compatibility: entries without the fingerprint field are existing v7.75 entries; entries with the field are v7.76+. The verifier dispatches on presence. The schema fingerprint is part of the canonical hash for v7.76+ entries, so any tampering with the fingerprint after signing is detected at the envelope layer.

The fingerprint is computed over the schema's *canonical bytes* — the deterministic serialization produced by `schema.MarshalParameters` or its equivalent for non-parameterized schemas. Two schemas with identical canonical bytes have identical fingerprints regardless of where they were published or by whom. Two schemas that differ in any field — including ordering, default values, or whitespace — have different fingerprints. The canonical serialization rules established in v7.75 (`parameters_json.go`'s round-trip discipline) extend to all v7.76 schemas without modification.

### 2. Content commitment primitive enables on-log facts about off-log content.

A new schema, `content-commitment-v1`, declares a Path A commentary entry that commits to off-log content under a specific schema reference. Domain Payload contains:

- `subject_pointer` — log position of the entity this commitment is about (typically the entry whose payload is being committed-to, or the log position of a record whose contents this commitment describes).
- `schema_ref` — log position of the schema governing the committed bytes.
- `schema_fingerprint` — pinned redundantly per Decision 1, allowing the entry to be self-describing without requiring a schema fetch for fingerprint verification.
- `commitment_value` — length-prefixed bytes carrying a 1-byte algorithm discriminator followed by the commitment digest.
- `commitment_purpose` — enum: `master`, `derived`, or `audit_snapshot`.

For v7.76, the commitment_value algorithm discriminator is `0x01` and the digest is computed via `LengthPrefixed("ORTHOLOG-V7.76-CONTENT-COMMITMENT-v1", schema_ref.Bytes(), serialized_bytes)` producing a 32-byte SHA-256 output.

The algorithm discriminator on `commitment_value` exists so future migrations to ZK-friendly hash functions (Poseidon, Rescue, Reinforced Concrete) can be expressed by bumping the discriminator (`0x02` reserved for Poseidon-over-BLS12-381, `0x03` reserved for future use) without breaking the v7.76 wire format. v7.76 ships only `0x01`; v8 readers handle multiple discriminators.

The `commitment_purpose` field is categorical and exists for verifier filtering rather than security. A verifier walking the log to find all masters under a schema queries for `purpose: master`. A verifier reconstructing a derivation chain follows the binding pointer (Decision 3) rather than searching by purpose. The field is informational; tampering with it does not weaken the cryptographic binding.

The content commitment is the foundational v7.76 primitive. Every other v7.76 schema either references one of these or composes them. Domain applications instantiate the pattern for their specific record types: a vital records office uses `content-commitment-v1` to commit to birth certificate contents; a court system uses it to commit to sealed filing contents; a property recorder uses it to commit to restricted deed bytes. The schema is domain-agnostic; the contents are domain-specific.

### 3. Derivation binding primitive provides cryptographic chains from master to replica.

A new schema, `derivation-binding-v1`, declares a Path A commentary entry asserting that two content commitments commit to the same plaintext under the same schema. Domain Payload contains:

- `master_commitment_pointer` — log position of the master content-commitment entry.
- `derived_commitment_pointer` — log position of the derived content-commitment entry.
- `binding_proof` — length-prefixed bytes carrying a 1-byte algorithm discriminator followed by the proof bytes.

For v7.76, the binding_proof algorithm discriminator is `0x01` and the proof is a Schnorr-style signature by the recorder's institutional key over the canonical concatenation of `(master_commitment_value, derived_commitment_value, schema_ref.Bytes(), schema_fingerprint)`, computed via `LengthPrefixed("ORTHOLOG-V7.76-DERIVATION-BINDING-v1", master_value, derived_value, schema_ref_bytes, schema_fp)` and signed over the digest. The signature is 64 bytes (32-byte R + 32-byte S for secp256k1 Schnorr).

The verification semantics are deliberately asymmetric: the binding is the recorder's *attested claim* that the two commitments commit to byte-equal plaintexts under the same schema. Anyone walking the log can verify the recorder produced the signature — the signature is valid only if the recorder's institutional key signed it, and a forged binding is detectable as a signature forgery against the recorder's published key. Anyone with one of the two plaintexts (master or replica) can additionally verify byte-equality by computing the plaintext's hash under the schema and comparing to the corresponding on-log commitment.

This is the v7.76 transparency property: cryptographic chain structure is verifiable from on-log data alone; byte-equality verification is asymmetric, requiring at least one plaintext. The full zero-knowledge variant — where byte-equality is verified without any plaintext access — is reserved for v8 via algorithm discriminators `0x02` (Plonk), `0x03` (Groth16), `0x04` (Halo2). The wire format accommodates the migration; v7.76 ships only `0x01`.

The discipline this enforces in production: a recorder cannot issue replicas with substituted contents without committing forgery. A corrupt recorder issuing a replica that does not match its claimed master leaves permanent on-log evidence of the false binding signature. An auditor with court-order access to the master plaintext, or cooperation from any replica recipient, can prove the substitution by hash mismatch. The cryptographic deterrent is bounded by the strength of the recorder's institutional key, which is the same trust anchor every other operation in the records system depends on.

### 4. Archival manifest primitive carries on-log structure for sharded records.

A new schema, `archival-manifest-v1`, declares a Path A commentary entry serving as the on-log wrapper for records under archival-sharded protection. Domain Payload contains:

- `record_identifier` — length-prefixed string for domain-side indexing (e.g., "TN-KNOX-BC-2026-04-29-0193"). The SDK does not parse this field; it is a free-form domain handle for the record.
- `schema_ref` — log position of the record-content schema (the domain's specific schema, e.g., `tn-vitals/birth-certificate-record-v1`).
- `schema_fingerprint` — pinned per Decision 1.
- `manifest_split_id` — 32-byte deterministic SplitID for the manifest's escrow split, computed via the v7.75 `ComputeEscrowSplitID(dealerDID, nonce)`.
- `manifest_commitment_pointer` — log position of the v7.75 `escrow-split-commitment-v1` entry for the manifest split (carries the Pedersen commitments for the manifest key's threshold split).
- `master_commitment_pointer` — log position of the v7.76 `content-commitment-v1` entry for this record's master contents.

The archival manifest is the on-log presence of a sharded record. Record contents live entirely in escrow (Decision 5); the public log only carries the announcement that the record exists, the schema governing it, and the cryptographic pointers to the manifest's escrow split and the master content commitment. An observer reading the log learns: a record with this identifier exists in this jurisdiction filed under this schema, with a manifest in escrow. They learn nothing about the record's contents.

The three pointer fields make the entry self-describing for verifiers. A verifier walking the log finds an `archival-manifest-v1` entry, follows `manifest_commitment_pointer` to verify the manifest split was committed-to atomically per ADR-005 §4, follows `master_commitment_pointer` to verify the record contents were committed-to under the schema. The three entries together — manifest, escrow-split-commitment, content-commitment — describe one archival record with full cryptographic structure.

### 5. Manifest escrow lifecycle wraps Pedersen VSS for variable-size payloads.

Two new functions in `crypto/escrow/`, `SplitManifest` and `ReconstructManifest`, compose the existing v7.75 `SplitV2` primitive with AES-GCM payload encryption to support manifest payloads larger than the 32-byte secret `SplitV2` accepts.

```go
func SplitManifest(
    manifest []byte,
    M, N int,
    dealerDID string,
) (
    shares []Share,
    manifestCiphertext []byte,
    manifestNonce [12]byte,
    splitID [32]byte,
    commitments []vss.Commitment,
    err error,
)
```

The function generates a fresh 32-byte AES-GCM key via `crypto/rand`, encrypts `manifest` under that key with a fresh 12-byte nonce, and calls `SplitV2(manifest_key, M, N, dealerDID, nonce)` (where `nonce` here is the 32-byte SplitID nonce, distinct from the AES-GCM nonce) to produce M-of-N threshold shares. Returns the shares, the manifest ciphertext, the AES-GCM nonce, the SplitID, and the Pedersen commitments. Each escrow node receives `(share_i, manifestCiphertext, manifestNonce)` packaged via existing `escrow.EncryptForNode` ECIES wrapping.

```go
func ReconstructManifest(
    shares []Share,
    manifestCiphertext []byte,
    manifestNonce [12]byte,
) (manifest []byte, err error)
```

The reconstruction calls existing `Reconstruct(shares)` to derive the manifest key, then AES-GCM-decrypts `manifestCiphertext` under that key with `manifestNonce`. Returns the original manifest bytes. The function zeroizes the intermediate manifest key before returning.

The asymmetry between share distribution and ciphertext distribution is by design: shares are unique per node (each node holds a distinct share of the manifest key); the manifest ciphertext is identical across nodes (each node holds an identical copy of the encrypted manifest payload). Reassembly requires M shares but only one copy of the ciphertext — any cooperating escrow node can supply it. The replication of ciphertext does not weaken security because the ciphertext is opaque without the key, and the key requires M-of-N reconstruction.

The honest-dealer assumption from ADR-005 §1 extends to manifest escrow: the dealer (the SDK function caller, typically a domain recorder) is assumed honest with respect to the polynomial randomness used in the underlying `SplitV2`. Adversarial-dealer scenarios for manifest escrow are deferred to the same future release that adds NIZK proofs of polynomial correctness for non-manifest escrow.

### 6. Authorization grant primitive records authorization decisions with explicit semantics.

A new schema, `authorization-grant-v1`, declares a Path A commentary entry recording an authorization decision. Domain Payload contains:

- `subject_pointer` — log position of the entry being authorized against (typically a record entry, but can be any entry that needs authorization-bound operations).
- `authorized_party` — length-prefixed bytes carrying the authorized party's public key (typically a 33-byte compressed secp256k1 key, but the field is length-prefixed to accommodate alternative key formats).
- `expiration` — 64-bit signed integer, microseconds since Unix epoch.
- `purpose_code` — length-prefixed UTF-8 string, domain-defined categorical value.
- `authority_pointer` — log position of the scope entity whose authority set governs this authorization.
- `identity_claim` — length-prefixed opaque bytes carrying a domain-produced identifier for the authorized party's identity proofing.

The signer of the entry (per envelope SignerDID) is the authorizing registrar — a member of the authority set declared at `authority_pointer`. The verifier confirms the signer's authority over the scope using existing v7.75 path classification and authority evaluation.

The `expiration` field is enforced by the verifier when downstream entries reference this authorization. Specifically, when a `derivation-binding-v1` entry or any other v7.76+ entry references an `authorization-grant-v1` entry as part of its chain, the referencing entry's `event_time` must precede the authorization's `expiration`. Mismatch is rejected at verification time. This makes expiration a cryptographic property rather than a domain convention.

The `purpose_code` field is opaque to the SDK — domain applications define their own vocabulary ("passport_application", "court_order_disclosure", "school_enrollment", "medical_record_request"). The SDK does not parse, validate, or constrain the purpose code beyond ensuring it is well-formed UTF-8 bytes. The categorical nature of the field is for downstream verifier filtering and audit pattern analysis, not for protocol-level enforcement.

The `identity_claim` field is similarly opaque. Domain identity-proofing systems (driver's license verification, biometric matching, knowledge-based authentication, bar license verification) produce stable identifiers; the SDK records the identifier on-log without interpreting it. The opacity is the privacy property: anyone walking the log sees the identity claim bytes but cannot reverse them without access to the domain's identity-proofing logs. The on-log presence of the claim serves as the audit trail; the off-log identity-proofing logs serve as the authoritative source.

A subtle but load-bearing semantic extension: `authorized_party` MAY be empty (zero-length bytes) when the authorization grant represents a *sealing* event rather than a *grant of access*. This pattern, used for sealed court records, encodes "this record is sealed; no party is currently authorized to access it." The expiration field carries the seal expiration date; the purpose_code carries the seal rationale; the schema accommodates both grant and seal semantics through the same entry type. The verifier treats empty `authorized_party` as a marker that no access authorization is currently active; subsequent grants with non-empty `authorized_party` against the same `subject_pointer` represent later access decisions.

### 7. Atomic emission discipline extends to v7.76 entry types.

The atomic emission invariant established in ADR-005 §4 extends to v7.76's archival record provisioning. The lifecycle function `BuildArchivalRecord` produces three entries — `archival-manifest-v1`, `content-commitment-v1` (master), and `escrow-split-commitment-v1` — together with the manifest shares for distribution. Either all three entries are produced and the shares are made available for distribution, or nothing is produced.

Implementation uses the same return-type discipline as ADR-005 §4: the lifecycle function returns a structured result containing all three entries plus the share package. A caller cannot extract the entries without the shares or vice versa. The mutation switch `muEnableArchivalRecordAtomicEmission` gates an internal assertion that all three entries are non-nil when shares are non-empty; flipping it false is a mutation-audit probe that exercises the "archival manifest without commitment" failure mode.

Replica issuance under v7.76 has its own atomic discipline: the lifecycle function `BuildReplicaIssuance` produces three entries — `content-commitment-v1` (derived), `derivation-binding-v1`, and a delivery-record `authorization-grant-v1` recording the actual issuance. All three are produced atomically or none are. The mutation switch `muEnableReplicaIssuanceAtomicEmission` gates the corresponding assertion.

The atomic emission discipline ensures that an external observer walking the log never sees a half-formed record (a manifest without its commitment) or a half-formed issuance (a binding without its replica commitment). The protocol's structural invariants extend to the new schemas without weakening.

## Scope boundaries

v7.76 does not modify the v7.75 envelope structure beyond adding the `SchemaFingerprint` field on entries that carry `SchemaRef`. No new ControlHeader fields, no new path classifications, no new entry-level security properties. The v7.76 schemas are domain payloads on existing Path A commentary entries.

v7.76 does not change the protocol version. Protocol remains version 7. The v7.76 schemas are schema-layer additions; existing v7 commentary-entry machinery covers their admission, witness flow, and verification without protocol-level changes.

v7.76 does not introduce field-level selective disclosure. Records committed-to under `content-commitment-v1` are committed as single byte sequences. A replica derived from a master is a re-keyed copy of the entire record; it cannot be a redacted subset. Field-level commitment trees, opening proofs for partial disclosure, and predicate proofs over non-disclosed fields are reserved for v7.77.

v7.76 does not introduce zero-knowledge proofs of derivation. The `binding_proof` field in `derivation-binding-v1` carries a Schnorr-signed assertion by the recorder, not a SNARK or Bulletproof. Audit-time byte-equality verification requires at least one plaintext (master from the recorder under court order, or replica from any recipient). Zero-knowledge derivation proofs that verify byte-equality from on-log data alone are reserved for v8.

v7.76 does not ship metering, billing, customer registries, pricing primitives, or operator-economics features. The SDK records cryptographic facts on-log; external monitoring, billing, and analytics services consume the public log to compute whatever they need for their own purposes. The SDK is neutral on prices, customer-DID mappings, and billing models. Operators implement their own metering against the public log using existing query primitives.

v7.76 does not ship proof-of-retrieval primitives for archival records. While "demonstrate that this record is still recoverable from escrow" is a real and useful primitive for audit verification, it requires interactive challenge-response protocols and additional cryptographic structure that is reserved for v8.

v7.76 does not modify the ADR-005 cryptographic locks. The universal `LengthPrefixed` rule, the canonical SplitID constructions, the wire-format locks for KFrag and CFrag, and the mutation-audit discipline all remain authoritative. v7.76 *uses* these foundations without altering them.

v7.76 does not modify the ADR-001 four-part legitimacy test, ADR-003 caller-provides-SDK-validates pattern, or ADR-004 zero-trust scope resolution. These earlier ADRs establish protocol invariants that v7.76 inherits unchanged.

v7.76 does not enable cross-operator share rebalancing. An archival record's escrow shares, once distributed to N nodes, cannot be redistributed to a different set of N nodes without performing a full re-provision (new master, new manifest, new shares, new on-log entries). Cross-operator share migration is reserved for v8.

v7.76 does not enable replica re-disclosure. A party receiving a v7.76 replica cannot issue a derived replica under their own authority; only the original recorder can produce derivation bindings. Recipient-mediated re-disclosure (where a school receives a verified replica and shares it with a school district under a derived authorization) requires additional cryptographic structure reserved for v8.

## Consequences

### Positive

Domain applications gain a complete protocol substrate for archival-grade record systems. Vital records offices, court systems, property recorders, and credentialing authorities can build production-grade systems on v7.76 without inventing new cryptography. The composition primitives — content commitment, derivation binding, archival manifest, authorization grant — cover the operational patterns these domains need.

Schema fingerprint discipline closes a real gap in v7.75. Schema substitution attacks — where an attacker modifies the bytes at a referenced schema position to reinterpret historical records — become structurally detectable. Every v7.76 entry that references a schema verifies the fingerprint at admission and at every subsequent verification. The cost is one additional 32-byte field per entry; the benefit is provable schema immutability.

Archival records support a fundamentally stronger threat model than v7.75 single-blob records. Contents never appear in public storage. An attacker who snapshots every public content store in the protocol's distribution mesh recovers nothing about archival records — those contents live exclusively in escrow node infrastructure. Compromise requires both M-of-N escrow node compromise *and* breaking the cryptographic primitives the manifest is encrypted under. The defense in depth is meaningful even against post-quantum adversaries, where heterogeneous-algorithm sharding (per the v7.77 roadmap) extends the protection further.

Derivation binding makes replica integrity a cryptographic property rather than an operational one. A passport authority verifying a birth certificate replica, a school verifying a transcript replica, a court verifying a sealed-record copy — all consume the same chain-verification flow. The chain is verifiable from on-log data using the recipient's plaintext; substitution attacks leave permanent on-log evidence; corrupt recorders are detectable through standard signature analysis.

Authorization grant primitive provides a uniform substrate for diverse domain authorization patterns. Vital records authorizations, court unsealing orders, property disclosure permissions, medical record releases — all follow the same on-log structure with domain-specific purpose codes and identity claims. Audit tooling written against the schema works across domains.

Atomic emission discipline extends the structural integrity of v7.75 to the v7.76 entry surface. An observer walking the log never sees half-formed records or partial issuances. The mutation-audit gates make these invariants machine-verifiable.

The wire format's algorithm-discriminated `commitment_value` and `binding_proof` fields enable forward migration to ZK-native primitives in v8 without breaking v7.76 deployments. v7.76 ships SHA-256 + Schnorr; v8 can ship Poseidon + Plonk by adding new discriminators while preserving v7.76 entry verifiability.

### Negative

v7.76 does not deliver full zero-knowledge transparency. The `derivation-binding-v1` Schnorr signature is a recorder's *attested* claim of byte-equality, not a ZK *proof*. Audit-time verification requires at least one plaintext to compute the schema-canonical hash and confirm match against the on-log commitment. Threat models where the recorder itself is the adversary — and no plaintext is recoverable for audit — are not covered by v7.76 alone.

Archival records produce three on-log entries per provisioning (manifest, content-commitment, escrow-split-commitment) versus one entry for v7.75 single-blob records. Replica issuances produce three additional entries (replica content-commitment, derivation-binding, delivery-record authorization-grant). For a high-volume domain (100,000 records/year, 5,000 issuances/year), this represents roughly 3.15× growth in entries-per-year for the records governed by v7.76 schemas. Operator storage and witness cosignature costs scale accordingly.

The schema fingerprint field adds 32 bytes to every v7.76+ entry that references a schema. For high-volume schemas (e.g., commentary-heavy domains), this is a measurable but modest envelope size increase.

The honest-dealer assumption for manifest escrow inherits the same scope as v7.75's plain VSS. A recorder constructing a manifest split with adversarial polynomial randomness cannot be detected by the SDK alone. NIZK-based dealer-honesty proofs are reserved for the same future release that adds them for non-manifest escrow.

Replica recipients cannot re-disclose. A school receiving a verified birth certificate replica cannot share it with a school district under their own authority; the school district must request its own authorization grant from the recorder. This is the correct property for most use cases (it prevents privilege escalation through forwarding), but it makes some domain workflows operationally heavier than they would be with recipient-mediated re-disclosure.

### Neutral

v7.75 entries remain readable and verifiable on their original paths. v7.76 is additive at the API level; existing domain applications consuming v7.75 schemas operate without modification. New v7.76 schemas coexist with v7.75 schemas in the same logs; verifiers dispatch on schema reference without protocol-level confusion.

The protocol version remains 7. Wire-format locks from v7.75 are preserved without modification. Cross-operator interoperability between v7.75-only operators and v7.76-aware operators is preserved: v7.75-only operators see v7.76 entries as well-formed Path A commentary entries with unrecognized schemas, and either accept them (treating the schema as opaque) or reject them at admission (operator policy).

External cryptographic audit of v7.76 covers four primitives: schema fingerprint discipline, content commitment construction, derivation binding signature, and manifest escrow composition. The audit surface is modest because each primitive composes well-understood foundations from v7.75. The audit timeline is decoupled from the v7.76 release: the SDK can ship with internal review while the external audit completes, and audit findings drive v7.76 patch releases or v7.77 corrections as appropriate.

## Phase gate

v7.76 closes when:

- `scripts/verify-phase-d-decomm.sh --full` exits zero, validating that every v7.76 schema, lifecycle function, and verifier passes its mutation-audit registry through the v7.75-established `cmd/audit-v775` runner.
- The manifest at `docs/audit/phase-d-manifest.md` enumerates every v7.76 schema, every new lifecycle function, every new mutation-audit gate, and every binding test, with dated pass entries.
- Global grep gates pass: `grep -rn 'muEnable.*=\s*false' --include='*.go'` returns zero across v7.76 source; `grep -rn 't\.Skip' --include='*_test.go' schema/ lifecycle/ crypto/escrow/` returns zero matches in v7.76 test files.
- An end-to-end integration test demonstrates the full archival record lifecycle: schema definition, master provisioning with atomic emission of three entries, escrow share distribution to five mock nodes, authorization grant by a registrar, manifest reconstruction from M-of-N shares, replica issuance with atomic emission of three entries, and chain verification by an independent verifier walking only the public log.
- An end-to-end integration test demonstrates the sealed-record lifecycle: provisioning under sealed authorization (empty `authorized_party` field), sealing notice on-log, subsequent grant of access via a court-order authorization, replica issuance, and chain verification.
- The release tag `v7.76.0-anchor` is applied.

Post-Phase-D work includes external cryptographic audit of the four v7.76 primitives (schema fingerprint, content commitment, derivation binding, manifest escrow) and any consequent ADRs if the audit surfaces issues. Domain applications adopting v7.76 consume the SDK as shipped.
